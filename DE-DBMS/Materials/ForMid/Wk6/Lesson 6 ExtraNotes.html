Concepts of the Entity-Relationship Model

  • Entity type, which contains entities
  • Relationship types, which contains relationships
  • Attributes 

The terms "entity" and "relationship" are commonly used when "entity type" and "relationship type" are meant.

Entities (like variables) have state, not behavior
Objects have state and behavior
Methods have behavior, not state

Examples:
Baseball: Player (n) ---  --- (1) Team
Company: Staff (n) --- --- (1) Branch
Bank:
   Loan (n) ---  --- (1) Customer
   Payment (n) ---  --- (1) Loan

Marriage could be:

  • an entity, for a registrar
  • a relationship, for a population census
  • an attribute, for payroll

    • Refinements in ERM:

    • Entity Type = ( strong | weak )
    • Relationship Type = ( strong | weak )
    • Cardinality of Relationship Type = ( 1:1 | 1:N | M:N )
    • Participation in Relationship Type = ( partial | full )

     Representation by Relations (Tables)

    • An entity is represented by a relation
    • A 1:N relationship generally implies a foreign key in the relation representing the N entity
    • A weak entity generally imports its key from the strong entity indicated by the weak relationship

     The Enhanced Entity-Relationship Model

    • Basic concepts of ER modeling are not sufficient to represent the requirements of the newer, more complex applications
    • Response is development of additional 'semantic' modeling concepts

     Additional concepts of EER model include

    • Specialization / generalization (two approaches to the same ER diagram)
    • Baseball: Pitchers and Hitters are both Players
    • Specialization: Players gives rise to Pitchers and Hitters
    • Generalization: Pitchers and Hitters gives rise to Players
    • Players = superclass, Pitchers/Hitters = subclasses
    • Attributes of Players inherited by Pitchers and Hitters
    • Subclasses may be disjoint or overlapping (can Pitchers be Hitters?)
    • A subclass represents a subset of its superclass (entities in a subclass always describe entities in the superclass)
    • Structure entities
    • Person: people are related to each other
    • PeopleStructure(PersonID, PersonID2, relationship)

     

    Refine ER Diagram

    • If an attribute is multi-valued, replace it with an entity and a relation.
    • If a relation has cardinality M:N, replace it with an entity and two relations.
    • If a relation has attributes, replace it with an entity and two relations.
    • If a relation connects K>2 relations, replace it by an entity and K binary relations.
    • If two entities are connected by a 1-1 relationship, (a) if participation is required on both sides, consider merging the entities, otherwise (b) consider introducing hierarchies.
    • Eliminate unnecessary subtypes by analyzing their attributes and relationships.

     Refinement of an ER diagram removes all obstructions to mapping into a relational model. After refinement, the ER diagram is effectively a logical data model.

    Terminology for the Entity-Relationship Model

    Entity Type An object or concept that is identified by the enterprise as having an independent existence.
    Weak Entity TypeAn entity type that is existence-dependent on some other entity type.
    Strong Entity Type An entity type that is not existence-dependent on some other entity type.
    EntityAn instance of an entity type that is uniquely identifiable.
    AttributeA property of an entity or a relationship type.
    Attribute DomainA set of values that may be assigned to a single-valued attribute.
    Simple AttributeAn attribute composed of a single component with an independent existence.
    Composite AttributeAn attribute composed of multiple components each with an independent existence.
    Single-valued AttributeAn attribute that holds a single-value for a single entity.
    Multi-valued AttributeAn attribute that holds multiple values for a single entity.
    Derived AttributeAn attribute that represents a value that is derivable from the value of a related attribute or set of attributes, not necessarily in the same entity.
    Relationship TypeA meaningful association among entity types.
    Relationship Type DegreeThe number of participating entities in a relationship.
    Recursive Relationship TypeA relationship where the same entity participates more than once in a different roles.
    Relationship Type Cardinality ConstraintsDetermines the number of possible relationships for each participating entity. Most common degree for relationships is binary with cardinality ratios of one-to-one (1:1), one-to-many (1:M) or many-to-many (M:N).
    Relationship Type Participation Constraints Determines whether the existence of an entity depends on its being related to another entity through the relationship.
    Relationship An association of entities where the association includes one entity from each participating entity type.

    ER Diagrams Dos and Donts

    A high-quality data model gives an impression of the business it represents.

     
    Five perspectives to check

    Conceptual correctness - does it represent the business concepts of the enterprise?

    Conceptual completeness - does it describe the full scope of the enterprise?

    Syntactic correctness - are the symbols used according to their definitions?

    Syntactic completeness - is the diagram fully developed in all its details?

    Enterprise awareness - is the model natural to the enterprise, stable and scalable?

    Name

    desirable features

    singular noun possibly with qualifier

    descriptive of the business entity being modeled

    reflective of the logical (not physical) nature of the entity

    undesirable features

    prepositions, conjunctions, verbs, and definite/indefinite articles

    abbreviations and acronyms

    references to individual real-world entities

    ambiguity (through homonyms and synonyms)

    Generalization Structure Checklist

    Create a supertype for a group of entity types

    of the same type

    with some attributes in common

    Create a subtype of an entity type when some entities have either

    a characteristic non-key attribute, or

    a required relationship with another entity type

    A subtype must have the same primary key as its supertype

    an entity can only be a subtype of one supertype

    Every supertype must contain or relate to either

    a subtype discriminator, for disjoint subtypes, or

    a role entity, for overlapping subtypes

    A subtype discriminator or role entity controls exactly one generalization structure

    A subtype can be a supertype (tree structure, generalization hierarchy)

    An entity can be a supertype for more than one generalization structure

     

    Structure Entity

    Aggregation hierarchy

    e.g., from (Product, Part, Component) to (Item, ItemStructure)

    Recursive relationships

    e.g., from (Person supervises Person) to (Person, PersonStructure)

     

    ER Model Checklist

    1-1 relationships

    eliminate by merging or by introducing a generalization

    1-N relationships with partial participation of the child

    eliminate by defining subtypes

    M-N relationships

    eliminate by introducing an associative entity

    Circular references (where entities are their own ancestors)

    check business rules and cardinalities and modify design

    Any pair of entities related by more than one dependency path

    check business rules and drop redundant path

    Any pair of entities connected by more than one relationship

    check business rules and modify design

    Primary keys

    each primary key has migrated in its entirety from the parent to each child

    no two entities have the same primary key (except subtypes and supertype)

     

    Normalization

    All levels of relational normalization apply to ER modeling

    1NF: move repeating attribute to a separate entity

    2NF: move attributes in 2NF-violating FD to a separate entity

    3NF: move attributes in 3NF-violating FD to a separate entity

    BCNF: to preserve FDs, modify choice of attributes

    4NF: factorize entity into two entities

    5NF: factorize entity into more than two entities

     

    Notes

    Points in italics in the entity, attribute, and relationship checklists will not concern us in this course.

     

    Instead of entity category as defined above, the we will follow the course text and use

    Type = (weak | strong)

     

    An artificial key for an entity is permissible when

    the entity is fundamental

    the attributes of this or other entities are sufficient for data access

    and either

    no attribute possesses all the characteristics of a primary key, or

    all candidate keys are large and complex, or

    the entity is part of a generalization hierarchy

     

    Attribute aliases are only permitted when an attribute is inherited more than once. For example, attributes in a foreign key should have the same names as the attributes in the originating entity.
