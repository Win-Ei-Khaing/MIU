CS422:  Database Management Systems
 
Lesson 12 - Distributed Databases

Purpose : - Gain the ability to discuss distributed database issues using the common terms.


A distributed database is a logically inter-related collection of shared data (or schemas) which is physically distributed over a computer network.
 

A distributed DBMS is software that permits the management of a distributed database and makes the distribution transparent to users.
 

A DDBMS incorporates local DBMS's which manage local transactions and data. The DDBMS may be homogeneous (local DBMS's are all the same) or heterogeneous (local DBMS's are different).

 
A DDBMS is a software structure with no relation to distributed or parallel processing.

 

Advantages of DDBMS

        Harmony with organizational structure (Dreamhome local offices).

        Data sharing with local autonomy.

        Improved reliability through data replication.

        Improved performance through data fragmentation, replication, and allocation.

        Facilitates modular growth: new sites added without affecting the operations of existing sites.

        Reduces hardware costs: a system of smaller computers is cheaper than a mainframe with equivalent power.

        Reduces connection costs.

 

Delicate points:

        Complexity of design

        Cost (?)

        Security of data and network

        Integrity: harder to verify constraints

        Lack of experience

        Lack of standards

        Degree of local autonomy

 

Fragmentation

 

The database is split into fragments for distribution. The fragments can be combined by union or join operations.

 

Correctness of a fragmentation scheme:

        Completeness: no data is lost

        Reconstructability: through operations of relational algebra

        Disjointness: no data duplication (except primary keys)

 

For example, a national real-estate company may have local databases in many cities (LA, FF, NY), and it may separate personnel records into accounting information (salary) and personal information (address).

 

Types of fragmentation:

        Horizontal: uses selection to create fragments (subsets of tuples)

        Vertical: uses projection to create fragments (subset of attributes)

        Mixed: uses both horizontal and vertical operations

        Derived: uses selections on relations derived from base relations by joins

 

A fragmentation predicate is a rule which determines a fragmentation of the data set. A set of fragmentation predicates is:

        Complete, if it produces sufficient fragmentation to match the principle units of business activity

        Relevant, if it does not fragment a body of data needed for a principle unit of business activity

 

Fragments may be replicated for reliability and speed. Replication brings with it a need for spreading modifications to a replicated fragment.

 

Fragment replicas are allocated to sites. The whole structure of fragmentation, replication and allocation is worked out to maximize efficiency and reliability and minimize costs.

The Ideal Distributed DBMS

        Keeps track of what data is where, and of the network structure.

        Updates all replicas on a schedule of replication intervals, taking care of communication and transaction failures.

        Provides transparency in:

        distribution

        location

        processing

        local DBMS's

        On the basis of:

        global external schema

        global conceptual schema

        fragmentation schema

        allocation (replication) schema

        ANSI/SPARC local DBMS's
